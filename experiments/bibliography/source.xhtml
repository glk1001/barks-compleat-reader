A Carl Barks Comic-Book Bibliography

THIS IS A bibliography of Carl Barks’s comic-book work — or, more precisely, a bibliography of his work for Western Publishing Company, which has published Barks’s work under a variety of labels (most often Dell and Gold Key) and through a number of subsidiaries (Whitman, K. K. Publications, Golden Press), and has itself changed names (it was Western Printing and Lithographing Company until 1960). Barks has written and illustrated comic books for no other company, but he has done some work for Western that does not fall in the comic-book category. Those items (children’s books, model sheets) are listed here because of their clear connection with his comic books. Whenever Barks stepped away from comic books to do something else for Western, the Disney ducks were always his subject matter.

Barks’s story credits at the Disney studio are listed in Appendix B, but no detailed description of the animated cartoons is included in this book, simply because it is on his comic books that his reputation rests. His work in animation has a claim on our attention because his work in comics was so brilliant; it is of limited interest in itself. The same is true of his magazine cartoons. Some future scholar may want to go through those old magazines, and compile a catalog of Barks’s work (which wasn’t always signed with Barks’s name), but I can’t imagine why; I think it is enough to know that Barks worked for the Eye-Opener, and to have seen a sampling of his cartoons (like those reproduced elsewhere in this book). This bibliography is restricted to Barks’s comic books because they are what really matters.

THE BIBLIOGRAPHY IS arranged alphabetically, by the titles of the comic books in which Barks’s work has appeared. (In listing both comic books and individual stories, I have ignored the ubiquitous “Walt Disney’s” and “Walt Disney Presents,” except when the Disney name is an integral part of a title, such as Walt Disney’s Comics and Stories.)

The bibliography covers all of the American comic books and related publications in which Barks’s work has appeared, through those issues bearing 1978 dates — which is to say, through those issues that had appeared on newsstands by the end of October 1978.

Except as noted otherwise, all of these comic books were, when Barks’s work was appearing in them, ordinary commercial comic books of the standard dimensions. When a title departed from the norm — by being a giveaway, or of an odd size, or something like that — that fact is noted at the start of the listings for that title. I have made no effort to distinguish “giants” from other comic books, giving instead the full page count for each comic book in which Barks’s work appeared. Likewise, I have not listed the cover prices of the comic books, since such prices have no meaning in today’s inflated market for old comic books.

Most of Barks’s work was done in eight-panel pages — four rows of two panels each — although he was very flexible within that basic framework. At the start of his comic-book career, he worked with six-panel pages — three rows of two panels each — and I have noted, at the start of the listings for Donald Duck, Our Gang, and Walt Disney’s Comics, how long his work appeared in that format until the change to eight-panel pages. Notes also identify those later stories that departed from the basic eight-panel format, usually by returning to the six-panel format. Barks has said that there was no particular reason for these later six-panel stories, “except that the office would ask me to try a six-panel format once in a while” (Apr. 7, 1968, letter to Michael Barrier). In 1945, when the wartime paper shortage was shrinking the size of comic books, Barks did a few stories in a cramped format of ten to twelve panels per page — a format that fortunately is visible in only about a half dozen comic books. As already noted, Barks’s work was published primarily under the Dell and Gold Key labels. Dell — an independent company, and not a subsidiary of Western — published most of the comic books that Western produced until 1962, when Western began publishing all of its comic books itself, under the Gold Key label. I have noted the labels under which a comic book was published, and also when the change in labels took place. When publication of a particular title was stopped by Dell and resumed by Gold Key, starting over again with No. 1, the listings are separate, rather than continuous.

FOR EACH COMIC book in which Barks’s work appeared, I have listed the issue number, the date, and the total number of pages, including covers. The first two items require some explanation. Issue numbers — Much of Barks’s work appeared in Dell comic books that were originally called “Four Color Comics,” and were numbered in that series. The Four Color designation was dropped after Four Color Comic No. 101; thereafter, these irregularly published comic books could be identified by the initials “O.S.” — for “one-shot” — preceding the issue number at the bottom of the first inside page. For example, this is what appears at the bottom of the first page of a 1949 issue of Donald Duck — “W.D.O.S.#223-494.” Translated, that means “Walt Disney one-shot No. 223, April 1949.” In the bibliography, I have listed all of these one-shots as “Four Colors,” even when they do not bear that designation on their covers, in order to clearly distinguish them from issues in regular monthly, bimonthly, or quarterly series. Dates — Most of the aforementioned Four Color comic books were not dated, except for the copyright date and the code date at the bottom of the first page; it is this code date that I have listed, in parentheses, since it tells more precisely when a comic book was published than the copyright date alone does. For example, Donald Duck Four Color No. 223 was published when Dell comic books dated April 1949 were being published; this means that it appeared late in February or early in March 1949, since Dell comics typically appeared on newsstands three to six weeks before the cover dates. Some comics in the Four Color series did have dates on their covers or in their indicia (a term to be defined later) in addition to their code dates. Donald Duck was published on a bi-monthly schedule within the Four Color series in 1951 and 1952. Some later Four Color comics had cover dates even though they were not published regularly; for example, Grandma Duck’s Farm Friends Four Color No. 1161 is dated Feb.-Apr. 1961 even though that title was never published quarterly. To complicate matters further, those cover dates were sometimes at odds with the code dates, so that the code date for Four Color No. 1161 is (January) 1961. (The cover dates apparently were advanced in this fashion in an effort to keep the comic books on sale longer.) In such cases, I have listed the code date as well as the cover date — again, with the idea of giving the reader a better idea of when a comic book actually went on sale. Gold Key’s code dates are on the covers of each issue under the Gold Key seal, so that “302,” for example, stands for either February 1963 or February 1973. A Gold Key one-shot or quarterly typically was put on sale three months before its cover code date, a bi-monthly two months ahead, a monthly one or two months ahead. (To muddy the waters still further, Gold Key dropped all dates except code dates from all of its comic books for a few issues in early 1973, even while continuing to publish them on regular schedules. This was evidently tied to a campaign to sell more comics in batches, in plastic bags. Topical material — like Christmas stories — was rooted out of the comics at the same time. In such cases, in the bibliography the dates are enclosed by brackets rather than parenteses.)

The “official” titles of some of the Barks one-shots, as given in the indicia, differed from the over-all series title; in such cases, the full “official” title of a particular comic book is given immediately under the line giving the issue number, the date, and the page count.

The entry for each story is broken down this way:

Story title — number of pages in the story — page numbers if they are given in the comic book itself — a capsule description of the story — date that Barks submitted the story to Western, or that Western accepted it.

Barks typically both wrote and illustrated his stories, but he sometimes worked from other writers’ scripts, sometimes edited or rewrote such scripts, and sometimes wrote stories that he did not illustrate. If a story (or front-cover drawing, or one-page gag) departs from the norm, that fact is noted in the entry immediately after the number of pages, by such terms as “art only” and “script only.”

Such notations are not based on speculation, but rather on a complete list of Barks’s work for Western that he made in 1969, for my use in compiling the bibliography. He updated that list through 1973, when he did his last work specifically for comic books. For each entry, he noted whether he had been paid for both story and artwork, or for story or artwork alone.

Barks’s list is chronological; the dates that accompany most entries in this bibliography, for submission or acceptance, are from his list. The dates for 1944 through 1957 are from Barks’s vouchers — the monthly statements sent to him by Western Publishing — and are, in effect, the dates on which Western accepted a piece of work. The dates for 1942 and 1943, and from December 31, 1957, through 1973, are the dates on which Barks submitted his work to Western, either by mailing it or by delivering it in person.

Starting in the middle fifties, Western began juggling the order of Barks’s stories and covers much more than in the past, as the amount of advertising in the comics grew. (No outside advertisements at all appeared in the Dell comic books until the summer of 1952, and it was several years after that before ads began appearing in the inside pages, as well as on the covers.) A story listed on a voucher as being for one issue might actually appear in another. Barks began keeping records of his submissions — often with descriptions of the work — in 1952, and his records are a more reliable source for the later items.

In addition to copying information from his records, Barks also checked this information against his own copies of the comic books in many instances.

In August 1978, Barks permitted me to examine the vouchers themselves, in an effort to clear up a few ambiguities in the list. In a fairly extensive examination of the vouchers, I found one or two minor errors in dates (and corrected them), but nothing more. I am sure of this: all of the dates in this bibliography conform to the dates on Barks’s list, and the dates on Barks’s list conform — with only a few possible exceptions — to the dates on his vouchers and his records of his submissions.

Unfortunately, Barks’s list is inconsistent and ambiguous in some respects, because of inconsistencies and ambiguities in those supporting records. For example, some stories and gags and cover drawings are described cryptically in Barks’s personal records, and he did not indicate, on the list, where he thought those items had been published. I have indicated, in notes accompanying the appropriate entries, when I think there is any reason to doubt that a date has been properly assigned to a particular item.

In some cases, a question mark is inserted in lieu of the day of the month; this means that Barks himself was uncertain of the date.

On Barks’s list, many covers are clearly identified as to content, or as to content and issue number, but some are identified only by issue number. In cases of the latter kind, the cover on a particular issue is probably the one that was submitted for that issue, but there is no way to be sure, since Western sometimes moved a cover intended for one issue to another issue. In addition, there are some conflicts. For example, Barks’s list shows that he submitted an unidentified cover for Walt Disney’s Comics No. 167, August 1954, on November 6, 1953, and another cover, identified as “fly on beak,” for the same issue on March 18, 1954. Since the latter cover actually appeared on No. 167, the November 6, 1953 cover probably appeared elsewhere, possibly on No. 168, for which no cover drawing is listed, but which does have a Barks drawing on its cover. Such conflicts are pointed out in notes to the appropriate entries.

With the assistance of Kim Weston — and, of course, of Carl Barks — I have done as much as possible to eliminate the inconsistencies and ambiguities in Barks’s list, and in the bibliography as a whole, but some remain, simply because the information needed to clear them up is not available in Barks’s records. I have tried to make the limits of my knowledge clear in the bibliography entries for such items. Perhaps some future Barks scholar will have better luck in unraveling the few puzzles that are left.

In any event, too much importance should not be attached to the dates in the bibliography, since the dates do not tell us specifically when Barks was working on a story, or even when he completed it. The dates are best viewed as a rough guide to the time when each story was written and drawn, and, even more important, as a reliable guide to the chronological order in which the stories were written and drawn. Barks said in an April 17, 1967, letter that he “ordinarily worked on only one story at a time. Never could comfortably mix them up or start a new one while one was still unfinished. The stories were a continuous chain-of-thought thing with me. They grew and were subject to change right up to the inking of the last panel. Besides, I liked a little mental rest after finishing a story.”

A chronological list of Barks’s work is included in this book as Appendix A. One can learn from examining this list that — for example — the last Uncle Scrooge story written and drawn by Barks was not the last to be published and his later stories in Walt Disney’s Comics were not published in anything like the order in which they were submitted.

In some cases, it is known — because the fact is noted on Barks’s 1969 list, or I noticed it while examining a voucher — that a particular story was scheduled for an issue other than the one in which it appeared. I have noted such schedule changes in the bibliography, even though most of them were insignificant, just to give an indication of how extensively Barks’s work was being juggled in the fifties and sixties.

One final point: to judge by the vouchers I have seen, the submission and acceptance of Barks’s work were usually separated by only a few days, a week to ten days at most.

In regard to the number of pages in Barks’s stories, pages were sometimes filled out with advertisements (usually house ads for other Dell and Gold Key comics), or with the Dell Pledge to Parents (Dell’s answer to the Comics Code, to which Dell and Western Publishing never subscribed). It is likely that in many cases, panels were dropped or cropped to make room for such things, but it is impossible to be sure exactly what happened in each case. Barks’s list indicates that he was usually paid for full pages, but that is not conclusive. Rather than speculate, I have simply noted, when the final page of a Barks story is filled out by the Dell Pledge or an advertisement.

Barks said in a 1974 interview that he did remember allowing space for the Dell Pledge “in the later years. I recall that they would send out a request maybe for a one-page gag, and they would specify a certain page was for it, it would have to have that ‘Pledge to Parents’ panel. That was on very rare occasions, it happened a few times, but it was not often. So much of the time, I was so far ahead of the publication dates that my stories had gone in long before any of these problems ever arose. ... They had to cut a story that they had already paid me for.”

The story descriptions are meant only to identify the stories, and not to summarize them. Sometimes, when stories are brief or simple, the descriptions double as synopses, but that is coincidental; I have tried only to give some idea of what a story is about, and to identify those major supporting characters (such as Uncle Scrooge, Gladstone Gander, and the Junior Woodchucks) who have significant roles in the story. When this bibliography was being published in installments, in Funnyworld, I published full plot summaries for some stories, and the general consensus — with which I agreed — was that those synopses were dry, boring, and superfluous.

When not otherwise identified, the quotations come from the aforementioned 1969 list of Barks’s work for Western. Barks refers to several persons in his notes: his daughter Peggy, now deceased, lived in Washington state at the time she was giving Barks ideas for stories; Chase Craig was an editor in Western Publishing’s West Coast office from 1949 until his retirement in 1975, and handled Barks’s work for much of that time; Dana Coty was a writer who worked for Judge magazine and in the Disney studio’s story department; Nick George was a Disney animator and story man who died in 1978.

“Changes” — This term embraces those changes made in Barks’s work after he had submitted it to Western — changes in dialogue, the deletion of panels or pages, and so on. Sometimes Barks was ordered to make such changes himself; sometimes Western’s editors made them.

“Weston” — These are quotations taken from an article by Kim Weston in Funnyworld No. 16, Winter 1974-75, on Barks’ unpublished work. The quotations are © 1975 by Kim Weston, and reproduced here by permission. The Weston notes accompanying the entries for Donald Duck No. 26 and Uncle Scrooge Four Color No. 456 include information about unpublished pages that had not come to light when the article in Funnyworld was written. The additional material in these notes is © by Kim Weston. Weston has made the most thorough study of Barks’s unpublished work, and I have quoted him in this bibliography because I saw no reason to duplicate his work — especially since my own findings agree with his. Some of the material that Weston discusses was deleted by Barks himself before his stories were submitted; Weston also discusses material that was (like material listed under “changes”) deleted or altered by Western, or on Western’s orders.

Other notes deal with unusual features that do not fit into neat categories — the introduction of a new character, the citation of a story in scientific literature, and so forth. I have not attempted to shoehorn all of my notes into neat pigeonholes, and you will find, for example, some quotations from Barks that are not flagged by the word “Barks” at the beginning of a paragraph.

“Reprinted” — Self-explanatory. I should add, however, that I have listed only those Better Little Book reprints that are in the “All Pictures Comics” format — that is, with panels from the original comic books reprinted one to a page. I have not listed those stories in which the comic-book artwork is on one page and the text on the facing page, since those versions strike me as too far removed from the originals to qualify as true reprints.

The entries for reprinted stories are also self-explanatory, I think, but I should say a word about the “changes” notes that follow many such entries. The “changes” listed here are the alterations that were made in the story as it was originally published. If, for example, a page was cut from a story before it was first published, and another page was deleted when the story was reprinted, only the second cut would be listed under the entry for the reprint.

For what I hope are obvious reasons, I have not listed the bits and pieces of Barks art that were reprinted, as one example, on the back covers of the March of Comics giveaway.

A FEW TERMS should be defined here, since they turn up fairly often in the bibliography.

“Indicia” refers to the information — the comic book’s title, number and date; the publisher’s name and address; subscription information; the copyright notice — that is usually published at the bottom of the first inside page of a comic book (and was published in the Dell titles, before they began accepting advertising in 1952, at the bottom of the inside front cover). The sentence “Panels on first page trimmed for indicia,” and variations thereof, which turns up frequently in the entries for the reprints, means that the panels were reduced in depth to accommodate the indicia at the bottom of the first page. (By contrast, in some stories the panels were increased in depth, because the indicia did not take up as much space as when the stories were first published.)

“New versions” — Barks only rarely duplicated the plots of earlier stories exactly, but there are many stories that seem to have been inspired by earlier stories. I have tried to identify as “new versions” only those stories that were unquestionably modeled on earlier stories, and have noted other stories that are highly similar to earlier efforts, if not quite identical in structure.

“Splash panel” refers to the half-page title panels that Barks used to open many of his stories.

SO MUCH FOR the mechanics of the bibliography. The really important question is, why is this bibliography so elaborate? A good question, because it is the people who buy this book who must pick up the tab for the filigree and furbelows that I have added.

The basic answer, of course, is that Barks is an important artist — to my mind, one of the three or four genuine artists who have come out of comic books, and easily the greatest of the lot. I think that a complete and accurate catalog of his comic-book work is fully as justified as, say, another scholarly study of an obscure eighteenth century poet. Not every item on this list is nailed down as firmly as I would like, because, as I have already mentioned, not even Barks’s records are a 100 per cent sure guide to what he did and did not do for comic books; there are notes in the bibliography that point up discrepancies between what Barks’s records say he did and the evidence of the comic books themselves. Question marks have been inserted when dates are uncertain or unavailable.

Despite such shortcomings, this bibliography is probably as accurate as such a bibliography can be, or is ever likely to be. When “Barks scholarship” becomes a minor industry late in this century, this book may at least prevent a few arguments in the pages of learned journals, and that is not a boon to be discarded lightly.

Granted, I could have boiled the information down to the point that perhaps thirty or forty pages could have been eliminated from the book; with tiny type, even more pages could have been eliminated. But I have tried to assemble a bibliography that holds some pleasures for readers, as well as for those who simply need a scrap of information. I think this is a bibliography that rewards browsing — that is filled with delightful surprises, especially in the form of insights into Barks’s thinking about his own work. It is worth knowing, I think, where the idea for the “Seven Cities of Cibola” story originated, and whose picture is on the wanted poster in “The Mummy’s Ring,” and what Barks thought about deletion of two pages from “Mythtic Mystery.”

Finally, there are the reprints. I have not enjoyed listing all of them, and I particularly have not enjoyed notating the changes Western has made in the reprinted stories. But for many readers — and especially for those who are just now coming to Barks — it is only through reprints that they can read his work, and I think they deserve to know not only where a particular story has been reprinted, but also how Western has altered it before reprinting it. If a splash panel is deepened or reduced in size, that is not a matter of great moment; but Barks was not careless about composing his panels, and I think that at least some of his readers will want to know if his compositions have been damaged.

At that, I have ignored some changes that really are trivial, such as very minor changes in dialogue and captions. In “The Second Richest Duck,” in Uncle Scrooge No. 15, for example, Scrooge complains that a newspaper costs seven cents; that was changed to ten cents in the reprint, even though equally outdated prices have been left alone in other stories. Likewise, dates have been altered in some stories, but not in others.

Along the same line, the color in the reprints is usually different from the color in the originals — but since the color did not reflect Barks’s intentions in either case, there is no point in mentioning the differences. The color work in Barks’s peak years was usually natural and unobtrusive, and that is about the best that can be expected from most comic-book publishers. The reprints are no better, but usually not too much worse.

The decline in the printing quality of Western’s comic books in the last few years is a more serious matter, because it robs Barks’s stories of some of their force. The highest accolade that can be bestowed by the more obtuse comic-book editors is that an artist’s work “reproduces well,” and Barks’s work has never reproduced as sharply as that of some cartoonists who drew more simply (and simple-mindedly). It suffers especially now, in these days of plastic plates.

This story is set in that section of Southern California in which Barks himself lived at the time. However, few of the settings were taken from life. He has said this about the settings for his stories, and “In Old California” in particular: “No settings are taken from life. But the type of houses and terrain are recognizably local. However, he said in the 1974 interview, “I was laying it right around San Jacinto there, where I lived. Now, this rock [the one the ducks’ car runs into, on the third page], that was just a couple of miles from where we lived. There’s a Soboba Indian reservation out there, and a road came across from San Jacinto, across a river and up here [to the rock], where it turned to go down to the Indian reservation. There were some rocks up there on the side of the hill — they weren’t exactly like that [like the one in the story], they were covered with sagebrush — and the Duck could very easily have made a miscue and run his car into one of those rocks.”

The supporting characters are humanoid dogs; Barks said in the 1971 interview that he would have preferred to have drawn them as real humans, but for the warning he had been given when he submitted “Dangerous Disguise” for Donald Duck Four Color No. 308, 1951. (see the note to that story).

DONALD DUCK in Trick or Treat - 23 - Donald refuses to provide treats on Halloween, and a real witch helps the nephews to counterattack until Donald finally gives in. (Mar. 31, 1952)

This story was based on a Donald Duck cartoon, Trick or Treat (1952), which was directed by Jack Hannah, once Barks’s partner in the Disney story department. The story was changed extensively before publication, as detailed below. Barks has explained the reasons for the changes: “Alice Cobb, the Western editor at the time, gave me photostats of the story board sketches and asked me to adapt them to a comic book version. I believe it was sort of open end as to length. Anyway, in the part where Hazel is trying to bust into Don’s closet, I departed from the movie script and added some business with an ogre that Hazel summons. [Barks’s departures from the film’s story were actually more extensive than that, as Kim Weston explains below.] Alice Cobb deleted the extra business and didn’t pay me for the unwanted pages. She was that mad.” Jan. 25, 1971, letter to Michael Barrier

Weston: “Trick or Treat” has the somewhat dubious distinction of the story with the greatest number of pages cut — ten and a half. More pages of art may have been drawn for it than any other Barks story (at least thirty-three and a half). It was originally a thirty-two page story that Barks had been asked to adapt from a Donald Duck cartoon of the same title.

After the title and credits, the cartoon starts with Hazel flying over town and around the church steeple, and that is how the comic story originally opened. The original splash panel is a view of Duckburg from a graveyard, and of Hazel heading for the steeple. The bottom half of published page 2 followed immediately. This splash panel was dropped, possibly for being too gruesome, and instead of just a different splash, one and one half pages were added to the story. One reason that has been suggested for this is that since this is a Donald Duck comic book, the title character really ought to appear on the first page, and as originally done, Donald did not appear until page 4 and even the nephews did not appear until page 3. It appears that the caption in panel 4 of published page 2 was either added or expanded to provide a transition from the added opening sequence. The story is then intact up to published page 16 (page 15 as originally numbered) and it follows the cartoon fairly closely, though the page 4 and page 10 sequences do not appear in the cartoon, nor does the page between panel 4 of page 16 and panel 6 of page 17.

Also, at this point comes a nine-page sequence (originally numbered pages 16 to 24) in which Hazel, following Donald’s comment on published page 16 that “It’ll take more than a WITCH to get candy from Donald Duck,” transforms herself into a beautiful girl duck (1½ page), gets candy from Donald (1), reveals herself as the witch, and hops on her broom with the candy and kids (½). Donald lassoes them and retrieves the candy (½). Hazel vows revenge and conjures up her pet, a six-armed, derby-hatted ogre named Smorgasbord, better known as Smorgie the Bad (1½). She sics Smorgie on Donald (½) and Donald tries to battle him off after noting that he’s a mighty solid figment of the imagination (2). On the seventh page of this sequence, Hazel directs Smorgie to find the key, and Smorgie, while still standing at the front door, sticks his arms through half a dozen windows around the house. Smorgie finally corners Donald, and Donald gives up (½). He gives Smorgie the basket of goodies, but then paints a stick of dynamite with jam (½), and gives it as a special treat to Smorgie, who gulps it down (½). Smorgie is exploded and Donald reclaims the candy, there being nothing left of Smorgie except a derby hat (½). The story then picks up with published page 17, originally numbered page 25, where Hazel can be seen stomping on the derby hat in panel 1. This whole sequence was added by Barks and does not appear in the cartoon.

From page 17 panel 5 to page 19 panel 5 (pages 25 to 27 by the original numbering), the story follows the cartoon fairly closely. But from page 19 panel 5 to page 20 panel 1, there is a break. At this point in the cartoon, Donald grabs a rug to anchor himself and keep from being dragged around too much and Hazel sprays a cactus plant which grows in size and smacks Donald in the tail, knocking the key out. Donald catches it in his mouth and swallows it again. Then Hazel has Donald’s feet swing south towards the fire to burn the key out, as in page 19 panels 6-8.

Barks apparently reversed the cactus and fire sequences, since the fire sequence appears on the bottom of page 19 (probably originally page 27). Judging from the below-mentioned page 28 (top) and the cartoon, I would guess that Donald didn’t originally regurgitate the key when brought to the fire (as published) but that the key was drawn in afterwards to provide a transition over the dropped page 28.

A half page numbered as the top half of page 28 is known. Its content follows: Hazel (spraying a cactus plant with witch’s brew which animates it) says, “Toughest knave I ever saw! He needs a partner!” and “Stomp along and don’t be lazy! That’s the ticket — bumps-a-daisy” (as the cactus plant backs up to Don’s tail and out comes the key.) Donald and a nephew race toward the key with Donald exclaiming, “I’ll not give it up! I’ll not! I’ll not!” I suspect that a half page (28 bottom) of contention over the key follows with it ending up in Hazel’s hands (published page 20, panel 1) but this final missing half page has not been seen. This would mean that pages originally numbered 29-32 of the story are intact and were published as pages 20 to 23.

Pages 20 to 23 (originally pages 29 to 32) basically follow the cartoon to the end but there are differences: in the cartoon, Hazel leaves the key on the floor and Donald just picks it up to slide it under the door. Donald is battered against the closet door head first in the cartoon, rather than tail first. The sequence in the top half of page 21 does not appear in the cartoon and no suit of armor is used in the cartoon sequence corresponding to pages 21 and 22. And finally, Donald does not appear at all in the cartoon sequence corresponding to the last page of the story.

When asked if the script had been edited so that the wolf in the story was not a real werewolf, Barks replied: “The thing you noticed about my ‘Loup Garou’ story is the sort of business I like to forget. Always there were decisions to make. Could a loup garou be a real werewolf? Could a witch be a real witch? How far can I stretch the ridiculous without getting in trouble with the office? How far can I push pure fantasy before some sophisticated 5-year-old kid complains that Donald Duck is only a fairy tale character like Hans C. Anderson’s people? I leaned toward logical explanations of phenomenons in everyday terms and mechanics — just to be safe. I hate to go back into those old stories and relive the struggles I had trying to make the explanations interesting and funny and not a dull let-down. Man! How I feel about a kid who yearns to make a career of writing and drawing comics — better, I say, he should take up something exalting and invigorating like sewer engineering.” (January 23, 1968, letter to Michael Barrier)

KITE GIVEAWAYS

(No number) - 1954 - 8 pages

DONALD DUCK Tells About Kites - 8 - art only - In a series of comic episodes, Donald teaches the nephews the rules of safe kite flying. (June 17, 1954, and July 8, 1954)

This booklet was published in at least two different editons, one for Pacific Gas and Electric Company and the other for the Southern California Edison Company. In the SCE edition, the third panel on the seventh page shows Donald telling the nephews to call the Edison Company to send a lineman to retrieve a kite that is tangled in power lines; the fourth panel (which is the width of the page, as wide as two normal panels) shows the lineman returning the kite. In the PG&E edition, the third panel shows Donald telling the nephews that they must notify PG&E about a broken power line; the fourth panel shows the ducks preparing to telephone PG&E; and the fifth panel shows a nephew writing down “Never touch fallen electric wires. Report them to P.G. and E. or to your parents” as one of his kite safety rules.

The final panel on the eighth page shows all of the kite safety rules. There are seven for SCE, and eight for PG&E, “Never touch fallen electric wires” being the extra one.

Barks’s vouchers show that Western accepted eight pages of pencil roughs on June 17, 1954, and accepted another eight panels of roughs and six inked pages on July 8, 1954. This would indicate that some of Barks’s pencils were inked by another artist, but there is no noticeable variation in inking styles in the story; all looks like Barks’s work. Since this was an unusual book, it seems likely that either his records, or Western’s, or both, failed to reflect accurately the work that was done.

Weston: By far the most important of the known unpublished Barks pages are the pages cut from “Back to the Klondike.” Originally the story was thirty-two pages. As published it was only twenty-seven. A five-page sequence was cut following page 11 of the published story, and another half page following published page 15. These pages change the characters’ motivation, making Scrooge a somewhat more sympathetic but more ambiguous character and Goldie less so. They tie up loose ends, contain some very fine episodes and gags, and reveal aspects of Scrooge’s character not previously known.

The deleted sequence begins after Scrooge turns to walk out the door of the Black Jack Ballroom (page 11, panel 8). The nephews trip him, demanding, “Don’t be so doggoned GREEDY! Tell us what happened when you met Goldie!” And thus begins a flashback sequence. Scrooge tells how he walked into the bar and ordered coffee, and how the sneers that followed stopped when he emptied his gold poke and out came the goose egg nugget, a hunk of solid gold bigger than his fist. “Suddenly Glittering Goldie was there in a long dive for the nugget!” “What a JEWEL!” she exclaims. “Don’t be so doggoned grabby!” replies Scrooge. Goldie hustles him off to a back room for coffee. “I don’t know what Goldie put in my coffee!” Scrooge reminisces, “but I awoke hours later in a snowdrift six miles from town! . . . drugged and rolled. Nobody can take gold from Scrooge McDuck! There’ll be a hot time in the old town tonight!” He walks back to town and stands in the doorway, doors swung apart. “Weaklings and cowards have ten seconds to clear out!” Goldie giggles. And then comes a fight to end all fights, which includes a beautiful half-page panel that Gary Brown once described as “like Jack Kirby with ducks,” an understatement if anything.

“When it was all over I cornered the star of the North on the balcony!” climbing up on a mound of bodies: “I’ll trouble you for my goose egg nugget, mam!” “She gave it to me but good! (right on the head) There was a girl with SPIRIT! But the other gold in my poke had been stolen! I made her give me an I.O.U. for it!”: “One thousand dollars, mam, at COMPOUND INTEREST, or I tear this dive apart splinter by splinter!” And he would have. Goldie: “There! Now GET OUT!” Scrooge: “I aim to, mam! But YOU’RE GOING WITH ME!” “I took her along, screaming like a singed cat!” Goldie: “Where are you taking me, you wolf?” Scrooge: “To my claim! You’re going to learn how HARD a miner works for his gold!” “And there at my diggings I made her work for a month!” Scrooge: “Keep digging, mam, or you don’t get any supper!” Goldie: “But I’m starving now! And my dress is in rags!” Scrooge: “You can buy a new dress when you get back to town! Buy it this time with money you earned SQUARE, not by drugging miners and stacking cards!” “When the month was up I paid her off!” “Fifty cents a day is all you earned, and it’s all you get!” “She threw the money in my face and stomped off!” Goldie: “I dug more gold than you did, tightwad!”

Thus end the four pages that are known to still exist. But internal structural evidence strongly points to the following conclusion: A fifth page was cut immediately following these four pages, and the four panels that form the top half of published page 12 were salvaged from that fifth page and used to connect published pages 11 and 12 while eliminating the whole flashback sequence. I suspect that nothing of real importance is in the eliminated panels, perhaps another panel or so to finish up the flashback, preparations for the trip, and a more extensive and scenic trip than is shown in the four remaining panels. Perhaps even those cut panels still exist, but I don’t know of anyone who has uncovered them yet.

Four and a half pages mean that the next several pages are made up of the bottom half of one page and the top half of the next, according to Barks’s original conception, so that his page design is broken up. For example, published page 15 is actually made up of the bottom half of the page originally numbered 19 and the top half of the page originally numbered 20. The bottom half of the page originally numbered 20 was also cut before publication. In it, Donald suggests going into town to get the law after this tough little old lady. Scrooge rejects that idea. He wants to keep the law out of this because he never kept up taxes on the claim; he really doesn’t own it any more than she does. Donald comments, “Oho! So you’re claim-jumping your own claim!” To which Scrooge replies, ‘Well, it amounts to that!”

Carl Barks explained the cuts in a letter to me dated February 17, 1972: the sequence “was cut out of the story at the editorial office in Beverly Hills in 1952. I was a little skeptical of whether I could get by with such a bar room atmosphere but I did it anyway for fun. Such pages, when cut from stories were never returned to me...the sequence was cut because of violence and dance hall atmosphere.”

[In the 1974 interview, Barks enlarged upon the reasons for the deletion of the five pages. When asked if he had been paid for those pages, he said, “Evidently I wasn’t...I think they figured I should have had sense enough to know you couldn’t get involved in fights in a barroom...It was quite a while afterwards before I found out why they cut it...I got a letter from the office, or was told on my next visit to the office, that I had violated a lot of their taboos and should have had sense enough to know it wouldn’t work...” One problem, he said, was the month that Scrooge and Goldie spent alone on Scrooge’s claim. “That was kidnaping, he picked her up and carried her out to his claim and made her go to work...It didn’t look very much like kidnapping yet it was.”]

Weston: I suspect that the message-in-a-bottle story in Uncle Scrooge No. 8 was originally planned as a thirty-two-page story but that Barks was dissatisfied with it and, at various stages, edited it down to twenty-eight. I know of one and a half unpublished pages from this story which went to fans out of Barks’s “scrappile.” And in reference to another story, Barks once wrote, “I often made such cuts in my stories whenever I detected any draggy spots. Usually this sort of cutting took place before the final inking,” but not always.

There are two unfinished two-panel strips (quarter pages) that fit with page 13. I say they are unfinished because they don’t have the solid blacks inked in yet, but they are otherwise complete. So they were probably edited out by Barks fairly early, before the pages were finally finished and numbered. One line is a flashback in which the Beagle Boy recalls, “We slithered in behind this cliff and all the boys but me went ashore for the raid!” One of the Beagle Boys pictured says, “Quiet! This’ll be a SNEAK job!” The next caption is, “They climbed the cliff! I watched ‘em! And then — And then —.” The other line has the scene back on the boat. Huey says, “WHAT?” and the Beagle Boy says, “ZOWIE! Just like that they turned to STONE!” Huey: “Yu expect us to believe THAT?” Beagle: “It’s the TRUTH! Somebody did it! Two other finished half pages are known. One, numbered 16 (top), fits neatly immediately after published page 15. Donald says, “I’ll help you, Uncle Scrooge!” and one of the nephews says, “Come back here!” Donald tells the nephews they are worrying too much and convinces the two with him to come up and help Scrooge. In the fourth panel we see Huey, still down on the beach questioning the Beagle Boy. There could be another half page between this and the published top of page 16. Or it is possible that the published top could have just been added to replace this one.

The other known half page would fit in place of the bottom half of page 17 (although that implies that another half page or more was probably cut between it and published page 18). In the first two panels, Scrooge says, “Sure enough! I can HEAR the ray bouncing off me — Zing! Zing! Zing!” “That means I’m safe while I get the boys out of danger!” The other two panels on this half page are identical to panels 5 and 6 of published page 17 but redrawn and with different shading. I suspect that what is published as the bottom half of page 17 was a half page that was redrawn to replace and condense a page or more in the original version. This story, I think, had a rough gestation period and required a lot of revising. However, too many pieces of this particular puzzle are missing to say exactly what happened. I feel reasonably sure that up to three more full pages from this story exist or did exist and were probably given away to fans by Barks many years ago.

Weston: In Uncle Scrooge No. 13, both stories were changed before publication. With this issue, an application for second-class mailing privileges was made (granted with issue No. 15). Apparently, the Post Office required that periodicals with second-class mailing privileges have at least two stories, with no single character appearing in both, and at least one page of text. That would seem to be indicated by the changes made in the issue.

“Land Beneath the Ground” was originally a thirty-two page story. The first cuts are made between pages 2 and 5 with the net elimination of two pages. And there is almost certainly some redrawing and changes in text. I say this for several reasons. First, some panels look like they have been relettered, or had captions or figures added or changed. Second, the natural rhythm and flow of a Barks story is not present in these pages but disrupted in such a way that I have long had a feeling that something just wasn’t right about the story. Now I think I know why — two pages were cut from the beginning against a tight deadline. Third, I think that if Barks had fully redrawn and rewritten these pages, he could have corrected the subtle imbalances that are there.

I think that part of the cuts (probably one page) occur within or between published pages 2 and 3. It is also possible that the first cut is an unknown half page that follows published page 3. These cuts could involve consultation with the professors at the university or a gag about paying the tunnel workers or perhaps Scrooge’s preparations for his own investigation after the workers flee, but this is strictly conjecture.

There are two known unpublished half pages, numbered 5 (bottom) and 7 (top), and one would think that there should be a full page between them. Unfortunately, there are three published half pages that fit very neatly between them. This rules out a simple explanation of these cuts that would place the order as unpublished page 5 (bottom), then an unknown missing half page, published page 4 and the top of published page 5, the top of unpublished 7, and then the bottom of published page 5. It also necessitates a more complicated and less plausible explanation. The top half to published page 4 could originally have been the top half of page 5 and in cutting and revising, the panel 1 caption could have been expanded, and Donald added to the picture. The kids’ dialogue in panel 1 and all of panel 2 could have been left unchanged. The dialogue in panels 3 and 4 and Donald’s expression would have been changed. He would have been just helping Scrooge load the mine cart originally. In the half page numbered 5 (bottom), Scrooge has the supplies already loaded in the cart. Donald asks him who he plans to have accompany him down as an observer. Scrooge asks Donald if he has $500 and coerces Donald into accompanying him down, using a 1950 IOU for 50 cents that—at compound interest—now amounts to $500. This would explain the pained expression on Donald’s face in panel 1 of page 4, a face that I think was clipped from elsewhere and added here. Next would come the bottom half of published page 4 but since it does not fit neatly in its present state, major revisions would have to have been made there. I can’t think of a better explanation except that Barks numbered the 5 (bottom) half page wrong (perhaps it was 4), but I am inclined to rule out that possibility. I don’t find this explanation of the origins of what is published as page 4 very satisfying.

Published page 5 (top) fits perfectly after published page 4 (bottom) and before unpublished page 7 (top). In 7 (top) the kids ask rhetorically who could have pulled the cable pin since there was nobody and nothing there except those round rocks. They conjecture about invisible men — invisible jokers playing unfunny jokes. The bottom half of published page 5 (originally numbered as the bottom half of page 7) follows and the story is unbroken through pages 6 and 7 (8 and 9 originally) up to panel 1 of page 8. The next nine panels (originally numbered as page 10 and the first two panels of page 11) are dropped and replaced by panel 2 of page 8, a panel added to make up for the nine panels dropped. The result is another page eliminated from the story length. In this eliminated page, the kids locate Donald and Scrooge behind a pillar, and still in the mine cart, which is looping around inside the arch. The kids suggest that they yank off a sideboard and use it to jam the wheels as a brake (as the nephews had done on published page 5). Donald asks, “How come WE don’t get any ideas like this?” Scrooge answers, “Don’t ask me! I only know how to make money!” The idea works fine, but unfortunately, the car stops at the top of the loop.

The story picks up with panel 3 of page 8 and appears to be intact up through page 20. On pages 21 and 22 there appears to be evidence of more cuts (misaligned panels are the usual evidence). So the final two pages of cuts were probably made between the end of page 20 and the beginning of page 23, though it is possible that a page was dropped somewhere else between pages 9 and 27. However only two panels of these two suspected pages are known to exist. In the first panel, Donald says, “More speed! It’s growing PITCH DARK up here! We can’t be FAR from the shaft!” and in the second, two Terry Fermians shout, “PUSH, YOU FERMIES! RAH! RAH! RAH!” “ROLL, YOU TERRIES! CHA! CHA! CHA!” This seems to fit with the top half of page 21.

The five pages were cut, presumably, to make room for the text page and the four-page Gyro story.

Barks had originally planned to do a story for Uncle Scrooge No. 34 with a plot revolving around an Aswan-type dam, but decided it would come too soon after “Pipeline to Danger” (Uncle Scrooge No. 30, June-Aug. 1960), another story with a Middle Eastern desert setting. “In its place I wrote a 16-pager of Uncle Scrooge and the ducks getting blown into Valhalla (a wandering small planet that strays into the earth’s shadow). You’ll have to read Uncle Scrooge #34 to see how I explain away such mythical anomalies with scientifically provable hogwash. As Louie expresses it: ‘Another of childhood’s cherished illusions reduced to so many nuts and bolts.’” (Dec. 30, 1960, letter to Willits.)

WALT DISNEY’S COMICS AND STORIES (Dell/Gold Key)

Walt Disney’s Comics was published by Western Publishing in its guise as K.K. Publications through No.328, January 1968,and was published by Western under its own name after that. Walt Disney’s Comics was never published by Dell, but all issues bore the Dell label from No. 93, March 1948, through No. 263, August 1962. Subsequent issues were published under the Gold Key label.

Barks’s stories in Walt Disney’s Comics were three panels deep through No. 41, February 1944, and four panels deep thereafter, except as noted.

Walt Disney’s Comics was issued in two editions, starting with No. 351, December 1969, through No. 360, September 1970. There was a twenty-five-cent edition that contained a poster, in addition to the regular fifteen-cent edition.

Walt Disney’s Comics, unlike most other comic books, was published in annual volumes, beginning with Vol. 1, No. 1, October 1940 — a carryover from the numbering system of its predecessor, Mickey Mouse Magazine. A new volume thus began each year with the October issue. The whole-issue number, as given in this listing, was usually found on the front cover, or in the code numbering at the bottom of the first page (for example, “WDC #100 - 491” means Walt Disney’s Comics No. 100, January 1949). More recently, it was also included in the indicia.

In December 1964, a ship carrying thousands of sheep sank in the harbor at Kuwait. Because of the health hazard involved (the city of Kuwait pumped its drinking water from the harbor), it was necessary to raise the ship rapidly, and Karl Kroyer, a Danish inventor, did so by filling the ship with expandable polystyrene foam. He sought a patent on the process, but, under patent law, a “preliminary description” had already been published, and, therefore, Kroyer’s process was unpatentable. That “preliminary description” was this story. Accounts of this incident appeared in Popular Science for April 1965, and Chemistry for September 1966, both of which evidently obtained their information (which differs in some details) from Danish publications. A few Barks panels are reproduced with each article. (The dialogue in the Popular Science panels is not by Barks; it evidently was re-translated from the Danish.)

Weston: For the taffy story in Walt Disney’s Comics and Stories No. 175, there are presently known one and a quarter pages of unpublished art. The sequence is numbered as the last two panels of page 6 and all of page 7 of the story, and begins with Donald running into the Akin-Bones Hospital calling, “Wait! Stop! Don’t anybody eat that candy!” Donald tells the nurses not to eat the candy because there is something in it, but he can’t tell them what. They think he is trying to trick them and he gets the candy back only by buying it at a dollar a bag. He eats it hoping to find the lost diamond ring, doesn’t, and soon meets Louie who gives him the good (?) news that he “sold all seventeen bags to the men at the boiler factory!” This sequence does not fit neatly in the space indicated by the page numbers, so rather extensive revisions probably were made, although no more artwork from this story is known to exist. Barks has said that he dropped this sequence because it wasn’t visual enough. The artwork found its way into fans’ hands from the Barks’s scrap pile.

Barks: “I have just finished drawing two ten-page Donalds for Disney Comics that were scripted by an outsider. Editor Chase Craig sent me the two scripts while I was busy drawing the Gyro #3. I welcomed them as a chance to gain some time. Besides, I thought the writer might be some beginner who’d be encouraged by having his stuff used. Well, after days of rewriting and gag-propping on the scripts, all for free, I got them in shape and drew the art. Then, too late, I noticed on the border of one of the sheets the barely legible name of the author. It’d been erased, but I could make out the moniker of an old hack who has been around the game for years, and has never caught onto the duck style. Boy, do I feel let down.” (Dec. 30, 1960, letter to Malcolm Willits)
